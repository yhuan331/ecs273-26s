Goldman Sachs (NYSE:GS) analysts haveestimatedthat Japan still has significant firepower to defend the yen after last week's currency-market intervention. Authorities likely spent about 5 trillion, or $31.3 billion, last Thursday to support the yen after it weakened past 160 against the dollar, according to Goldman economist Yuriko Tanaka. That move suggests Japanese policymakers may see the 160 level as a possible line of defense, especially because the intervention came during relatively moderate market volatility. Goldman estimated that Japan could intervene roughly 30 times at last week's scale, though officials are expected to be careful with their reserves and choose moments when each move could possibly have the strongest impact. Tanaka indicated that the Ministry of Finance may aim to maximize the impact of future intervention because its funding source is finite. That means officials could be more likely to act when the yen is weakening quickly, rather than spending aggressively every time the currency comes under pressure. The latest move followed repeated warnings from officials and marked Japan's first intervention since July 2024, when the yen had also crossed the 160 level and then surged after authorities stepped in. The yen has remained under pressure because of high energy costs and expectations that the gap between U.S. and Japanese policy rates may not narrow soon. For investors, the next phase could be less about whether Japan has enough reserves and more about when officials decide the timing is worth the risk. Tanaka said the likelihood and scale of further yen-support moves will depend on the pace of depreciation, market volatility and outright currency levels. Japan held about $1.2 trillion in foreign reserves at the end of March, including $161.7 billion in foreign-currency deposits that can be used for intervention, while the rest is held in foreign securities. Bloomberg strategists noted that Japanese authorities targeted the London-European session last week, but with the U.K. on a public holiday Monday, attention could shift toward New York.
