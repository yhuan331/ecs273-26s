Exxon Mobil’s analyst fair value estimate has been nudged higher, moving from US$164.13 to US$165.64 per share. Behind this shift is a mixed but slightly more constructive analyst stance, with higher and lower price targets clustering around updated oil price assumptions, cash flow expectations, and views on whether the current valuation already reflects a supportive backdrop. As you read on, you will see how to track these changing signals and what to watch to stay aligned with the evolving Exxon Mobil story. Analyst Price Targets don't always capture the full story. Several firms have lifted Exxon Mobil price targets in recent months, including Piper Sandler to US$186, Wells Fargo to US$183, and Mizuho to US$162. These changes reflect models that factor in higher assumed oil prices and cash flow potential. Barclays, Citi, BofA, HSBC, Bernstein, Morgan Stanley, TD Cowen, JPMorgan, RBC Capital, Jefferies, BMO Capital, Mizuho and UBS have all raised targets at various points. Their updates are often tied to revised commodity price outlooks and views on the stock’s capacity to return cash to shareholders. Some bullish research, such as from Wells Fargo and Piper Sandler, links higher valuation frameworks to expectations around return of capital durability and tighter crude balances in analysts’ supply and demand models. Wolfe Research has downgraded Exxon Mobil and also cut its target in a separate report. TD Cowen, Morgan Stanley and Piper Sandler have issued target trims at different dates, signaling caution around how current pricing and macro assumptions feed into their valuation work. Rating changes from firms like Wolfe Research and BNP Paribas show that some analysts see the risk that recent oil price assumptions and conflict related premiums may not fully translate into sustained upside for the stock. Reports indicate Exxon Mobil is exploring a sale of its Esso branded gas station network in Hong Kong, valued at up to US$600m, as part of a wider portfolio review focused on core operations and energy transition projects. Exxon Mobil is believed to have hired Goldman Sachs to evaluate a potential exit from the New Zealand market, with the business reportedly expected to raise between US$500m and US$1,000m and potentially be sold in parts. In offshore Guyana, Exxon Mobil and partners Halliburton and Noble completed what is described as the industry’s first fully automated geological well placement with complete rig automation, using closed loop drilling and geosteering technology. Media reports say Exxon Mobil plans to move its legal home to Texas from New Jersey, while its Australian unit, Mobil Oil Australia, was fined A$16m after admitting to misleading claims about fuel additive content at certain Queensland petrol stations.
